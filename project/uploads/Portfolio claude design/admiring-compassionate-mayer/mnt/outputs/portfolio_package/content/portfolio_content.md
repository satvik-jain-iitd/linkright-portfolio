## SECTION 2: THE STORY

### Chapter 1 — The Foundation

My father died when I was 11.

I'm not starting with this for sympathy. I'm starting here because it's the most honest explanation for how I operate. When something important breaks, you figure out who's responsible — and you become that person.

My family didn't fall apart. It held. My mother worked. My sister studied. And I watched, very closely, how systems either support people during hard moments or quietly fail them. That question — *why do some systems work and others collapse* — became the lens I've used to look at every problem since.

---

### Chapter 2 — The Theory vs. Practice Problem

IIT Delhi was academically hard for me. I graduated with a 6.43 CGPA in Civil Engineering — not a number I hide.

What I can also tell you: I scored 10/10 in every single practical lab I ever sat in. That gap wasn't about intelligence. It was about how I learn. I don't absorb things from textbooks. I absorb them by doing. When the problem is real and the stakes are present, something shifts.

This pattern has followed me everywhere. The Sprinklr Gold Medal. The AmEx roadmap built from scratch. LinkRight, shipped while holding a full-time job. The fastest I've ever grown is when the problem was slightly too large for me and I had to figure it out on the way.

---

### Chapter 3 — The Gold Medal Nobody Saw Coming

Six months into my first real job, my director put my name on a slide in a global all-hands meeting.

Number one. Out of every implementation consultant at Sprinklr worldwide. 10/10 customer satisfaction.

I wasn't chasing a medal. I was just trying not to drown in my first enterprise engagement. The award mattered less than what it proved to me: that when I'm in an environment where I can apply what I know *practically*, I operate at a very high level.

What followed was the biggest deal Sprinklr had ever signed — a $32 million engagement with the Government of Qatar. Forty ministries. Citizens interacting with public services. Arabic-language dashboards for the Prime Minister's office. A team that grew from 6 to 14 people. And 120 dashboards that needed to be right the first time, in 4 weeks.

Then came Walmart — building a real-time system that could tell the company why its gig drivers were churning, while the data was still arriving. I spent a Black Friday midnight pulling live screenshots from dashboards and narrating what they meant to a room full of VPs. No script. The system was working. Someone had to translate it into language humans could act on. That was me.

Those two years taught me something that no PM course covers: the best products are built by people who stay in the room when it gets messy, not just when it's clean.

---

### Chapter 4 — Solo Ownership

When I joined American Express in 2024, I was the only product manager on my team.

No one above me. An 18-person engineering team that needed direction. A document with 21 requirements and no roadmap.

I spent the first few weeks talking to compliance analysts across 6 global regions — trying to understand what people actually needed before I touched any plan. By week 6, I had a 3-year roadmap. By week 8, the SVP of Risk Management gave me an award for it. I hadn't shipped a single feature yet.

Then I ran 10 consecutive sprints. Alone. And then, twice in the same year, the strategy changed completely from under me.

The first time: the entire methodology we'd been building toward was reversed midway. The second time: the platform I'd spent months building was right at the edge of production — and got paused. Both times, the team was watching how I'd respond. Both times, we were back in motion within a week.

What I learned wasn't a technique. It was something simpler: the value of a product manager is directly proportional to how fast they can reorient *without losing the team*.

---

### Chapter 5 — Building Things That Matter

LinkRight started as a tool I built for myself.

I was running a job search and the AI tools available were doing something that bothered me — they were making me sound like someone I wasn't. Polished. Inflated. Unrecognizable.

So I built something different. Before I wrote a single line of code, I spent months talking to 147 people and reaching out to over 7,000 more to understand whether this problem was uniquely mine. It wasn't.

What came out of that is a career navigation system that knows who you actually are — and helps you apply from that real place, not a fabricated one. I'm still building it, while holding my AmEx role full-time.

But the thing that means most to me this year isn't a product.

It's the colleague who came to stay with me after a layoff. First-generation engineer, brilliant, no interview confidence. We ran mock sessions together for 3 months — 15 to 20 full practice interviews. He secured an APM role without a single day of unemployment between jobs.

Building something is great. Watching someone go from zero confidence to performing at their ceiling is a different kind of impact entirely.

### Case Study 02 — Walmart Driver Retention (GenAI)
**The problem:** Walmart's gig driver platform was losing most of its drivers within 2 months. Nobody knew why. Contact volume was over 100,000 cases. The data was spread across 6 completely different sources.

**What I did:** Built a root cause analysis product that unified all 6 sources into a single picture — and used machine learning to automatically surface the patterns behind driver churn. Designed the full taxonomy, ran the architecture, led weekly updates to Walmart's VP of Product.

**What changed:** Time to insight went from 7 days to same-day. The POC converted to a $1.2M deal with $9M+ in follow-on pipeline.
